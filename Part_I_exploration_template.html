varnames = ['radius_mean', 'perimeter_mean', 'area_mean', 'compactness_mean','texture_mean']
binsizes = [0.5, 5, 50,0.005, 0.5]
def plot_histograms(df, varnames, binsizes):
    # Loop through each variable
    for i, varname in enumerate(varnames):
        # Calculate bins using the corresponding binsize
        bins = np.arange(0, df[varname].max()+binsizes[i], binsizes[i])
        
        # Create histogram using Matplotlib's hist function
        plt.hist(df[varname], bins=bins)
        
        # Add labels and title
        plt.xlabel(varname)
        plt.ylabel("Count")
        plt.title(f"Histogram of {varname}")
        
        # Show the plot
        plt.show()

plot_histograms(df, varnames, binsizes)

This distribution of the radius_mean looks has a look of a normal distribution, but drags on to be the right to make it right skewed. It could be seen that majority of the values fall between 10 to 20, with a slight drop in between.

This is rather interesting, having quite a number of values start between 15 to 25 before having right skewed look,starting from 50 ending at the 175 value. Could the earlier values be incorrect value recording or just unique values?

Area mean is glaringly right skewed with a bit of drag,which does ends in one view, thus not necesitating a log plot. It however have disparate distrubtion going from the 1500 value updwards.

Just like the usual right skew trend, the compactness measurement looks to follow the others. The peak values from this looks to be from the 0.05 to 0.12

Out of all the considered variables, this is the closest thing to normal distribution. This could probably be because texture had values have range of values which are predifined values which the instrument of measurement chooses.

def plot_diagnosis_vs_feature(df, feature):
    # Create a pointplot using Seaborn
    sb.pointplot(x='diagnosis', y=feature, data=df, hue = 'diagnosis',
           palette = 'Blues', linestyles = '', dodge = 0.8)
    
    # Add labels and title
    plt.xlabel('Diagnosis')
    plt.ylabel(feature)
    plt.title('Diagnosis vs. {}'.format(feature))
    
    # Show the plot
    plt.show()
plot_diagnosis_vs_feature(df, 'radius_mean')
plot_diagnosis_vs_feature(df, 'area_mean')
plot_diagnosis_vs_feature(df, 'perimeter_mean')
plot_diagnosis_vs_feature(df, 'compactness_mean')

Yes, malignant cells have higher values followed by pre-malignant values, with benign cells having the least radius. However, pre-malignant cells are seen to have the widest range of radius span than the other two,which shows it contains more random measurement than the other two.

It follows again, that malignant cells have the largest value of areas. This time round though, benign cells follow up and pre-malignant cells have the least values. Yet it is confirmed again that it contains more random and wide measurement of values

The trend follows similarly for perimeter and compactness. This confirms that, pre-malignant cells are randomly distributed, but benign cells are smaller in sizes based radius,area,perimeter and compactness whiles malignant cells are larger in sizes on these same criterias

Interesting findings¶

A general relationship observed, which was surprising was that, I expected the pre-malignant cells to have had features in between benign and malignant. However,it turned out it was not direct an did not have a definite relationship. This means more care and experience is needed to declare it as pre-malignant.

Conclusions¶

It was observed that generally, benign cells had smaller radius, smaller perimeter, smaller area and compactness than malignant cells. Larger cell measurement values suggest strong likelihood of malignant cells and less values of measurement suggest benign cells.

More than half of the cells were benign, yet an significant number of the cells, i.e 35.1% of the cells are malignant.

The measurement values of radius, perimeter, area, concavity, and concavity of the dataset have a significant degree of correlation with one another suggesting multicollinearity.
